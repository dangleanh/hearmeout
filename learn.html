SIGN LANGUAGE

Today, sign language used in Singapore is a continually developing blend of Shanghainese, American and locally-generated signs. It comprises different types of systems, including SEE-II, Pidgin Signed English (PSE) and gestures. SgSL is socially recognised and accepted by the deaf community in Singapore, and is a reflection of Singapore's diverse linguistic culture.

BASIC WORDS
MYTHS
Deaf people cannot speak.

The Deaf Community is made out of vastly different individuals. Some speak as well as you and me, while others choose not to communicate verbally if they think they might not be understood or are not able to properly gauge their pitch and volume.

Lack of hearing does not make one a bad driver. After all, there is no hearing aspect in license testing. In fact, Deaf or Hard of Hearing individuals are proven to be better drivers than Hearing people. This is because Deaf individuals are more observant than Hearing individuals.

Deaf people cannot drive.
Deaf people are less intelligent.

Hearing loss has no bearing on intelligence levels. A Deaf person can do everything you and I can do, except hear as well. Just as with Hearing people, there are many Deaf people who have achieved great success.

Much as with myth #1, this is based on preference. Some people choose to sign, while others do not. There are many ways deaf people communicate such as signing, lip reading, a combination of both, gesturing, writing or the use of an interpreter.

All Deaf people sign.
ETIQUETTES

Respect
Deaf individuals deserve as much respect as you and me. Just because they cannot hear as well as a Hearing person, doesn’t mean they deserve less regard. In fact, many Deaf individuals would rather be treated the same as everyone else. One with good intentions may make special arrangements for a Deaf individual but this may make him or her feel uncomfortable. Remember, a person’s level of hearing does not define him or her. Talking to a deaf person in a friendly way is the best approach.

Talk to the individual, not the interpreter
Sometimes, Deaf individuals use interpreters to help them converse. It is important to remember that even with an interpreter, the Deaf person is the one taking part in the conversation. Hence, one should talk directly to the Deaf individual, by facing him or her.

Getting their attention
It is not necessary to execute exaggerated motions to get a Deaf person’s attention. A tap on the shoulder or a quick hand wave is enough.

Lip reading
Not all Deaf people lip-read. For those who do, they require the other party to speak distinctly. There is no need to speak in an unnaturally slow manner, or to shout, but speaking clearly will help the Deaf individuals understand better.
